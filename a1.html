Circuit:

My circuit for A1 features three LED lights connected to my Arduino Uno via ports D13, D8, and D7. I needed to calculate the resistance for each of these LEDs. We know that yellow, red, and green LEDs all experience 1.8 V drop. After some research, I found that the Arduino Uno opporates at 5 V. V = I x R. We know I = 0.02. We know V = 5 - 1.8 (3.2). 3.2 = 0.02 x R, R = 3.2 / 0.02, R = 160 Ohms. I dont have a 160 Ohm resistors, so my circuit features 100 Ohm resistes.

"Blinking" Operation:

For my project, I decided to use the green, yellow, and red lights to envoke the feelings associated with a traffic light. I thought it was a fun (albeit predicatable) color scheme to base my blinks on. For the pattern itself, I actually had my roommate describe to me a blinking pattern that he would like to see, and I based the blinking on that. This is because, as designers, our creativity is often dictated by project managers, so I simmulated this in A1.

Questions:

Draw a graph where the X axis is time and the Y axis is voltage across the LEDs. Draw a line for each of your 3 LEDs.

How many LEDs could you blink at once with your Arduino? How much current would that draw?

According to the Arduino Uno data sheets, the current limitation of the Arduino Uno is 200mA. As each LEDs current draw is 20mA, we can assume that we could blink up to 10 LEDs at the same time before topping out the current limitation (20mA x 10 = 200mA).

How many LEDs could you blink with your Arduino if only one were to be on at once?

There are 14 digital pins (0-13) and 6 analog pins (a0-a6) on the Arduino Uno. Because each LED will be lit individually, we don't have to worry about the current draw of each one. 14 + 6 results in 20 total LEDs that could be individually "blinked".

How fast do you need to blink your LEDs until you no longer can tell that they are blinking?

I went about this problem in probably the least scientific way possible, but I am not able to attend class monday so I am not sure of a better way to do this. I used the Arduino IDE and set up a program that flashed the light for a tenth of a second. This was an obviously flashing light. I adjusted it to 0.05 seconds. Still flashing. The same was true at 0.025. Finally, me and the group of friends I was with descided that flashing for 0.009 secnds was the slowest blink we were unable to see. 60 / 0.009 = 6666.67, so that is roughly how many times you have to blink a light in a minute before you can no longer tell.
